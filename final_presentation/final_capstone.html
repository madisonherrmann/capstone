Welcome to our presentation on ‘Machine Learning in Digital Mental Health Interventions.’ Today, Madison Herrmann and I, Tianrun Zhu, will discuss how advanced computational methods are applied to personalize and enhance mental health care using digital platforms.

Alcohol and opioid use disorders are chronic conditions that require lifelong management. Recovery is often a long, dynamic, and complex process, with individuals facing numerous challenges:

Effective initial treatment is not enough—sustained support is critical but often lacking.

Recovery needs continuous monitoring because what works at one moment might not work at another.

Individuals’ needs evolve over time, adding complexity to long-term recovery management.

There is an unmet need for accessible and affordable solutions to address these challenges effectively.

Predicting Lapse in OUD

  • Predict day-by-day probability of future lapse


  • Label windows roll day-by-day starting at 6am in the participants’ time zone.


  • Take the daily EMA data.

Using daily EMA data, we performed feature engineering to transform raw data into meaningful signals predictive of future opioid lapse. Machine learning models were then developed to predict the likelihood of a lapse occurring on the following day.

Models

  • XGBoost algorithm

    • optimized using the Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP) method


  • 5x10 k-fold CV


  • CHTC

The models were trained using the XGBoost algorithm with a five by ten-fold cross-validation framework to ensure robust performance evaluation. We used XGBoost because it consistently outperformed the other three algorithms during preliminary exploratory analyses, including Random Forest, K-Nearest Neighbors, and Elastic Net. Furthermore, the Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP) method, which we planned to use for explanatory analyses of feature importance in our full models, is optimized for XGBoost. Model training utilized high-throughput computing to efficiently explore and identify the optimal combination of hyperparameters.

Model Performance

  • The best model achieved an outstanding area under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (auROC) of 0.94

Given the imbalance in our labels, with a large majority of non-lapse cases and a small portion of lapse cases, we used the area under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (auROC) to evaluate model performance, as it is effective in handling unbalanced data. The right graph shows the auROC values of the best model across the 5 by 10, that is 50 folders. The median auROC is 0.94, which is outstanding. This means there is a 94% chance that the model will score a lapse higher than a no-lapse when it is a lapse instance. The left shows a graph of the roc curve for the best model.

Model Performance

This model achieved a high fairness across different demographic groups. As is shown in the graphs, the differences in median auROC for different gender, race and income groups are very small, and for all groups, the auROC scores are high indicating good performance.

Model Implementation

  • How might we use these models to deploy the right recommendation, to the right person, at the right time?

  • Recent NIH-funded Grant:

    • Optimizing Message Components of a Recovery Monitoring Support System for Engagement and Clinical Outcomes for Alcohol Use Disorder
      • Interventions derived from Relapse-Prevention Model (Marlatt & Gordon, 1985)

Now that we have a model that can successfully predict lapse probability, How might we implement it to deploy the right recommendation, to the right person, at the right time? A recently funded grant aims to explore how to use these models for clinical benefit, in an effort to build a recovery monitoring support system. This system will deliver tailor made recommendations based on what unique features are impacting lapse risk for that individual, at that particular moment. Output from machine-learning models will be anchored within the Relapse Prevention model to identify the specific intervention and supports that are risk-relevant for each individual. For example, during sensed periods of high stress, guided mindfulness could be recommended as an intervention. If increased time at risky locations is driving lapse risk, the system could provide information on local AA or other support meetings. Continuous monitoring of risk features is essential to deploy the necessary intervention, for that individual, at that particular moment in time.

Area under ROC Curve (auROC)

I retrained the model using 1x daily EMA with geolocation and day level windows. High throughput computing was utilized along with the XGBOOST algorithm and 5 by 10 kfold cross validation. The best configuration resulted in a median auroc of .85. The drop in performance is likely due to using 24-hr periods instead of hour-by-hour windows to predict lapse probability. Despite the drop in performance, additional geolocation improved the models contextual awareness, a key step that brings us closer to deploying a real-time risk monitoring and support system. Important Risk Features

Future efficacy asked participants how likely are you to drink within the next week, on a scale from 1-10? On average, self-reports of high likelihood were associated with a higher probability of lapsing.

Important Risk Features

Risky location is a gps feature where participants classified frequently visited locations as low, medium, high, or no risk. Through GPS monitoring, the time spent at these locations was recorded. It’s likely that time spent at a high risk location, such as a bar, would increase lapse probability. Whereas, time spent at no risk location, such as an AA meeting, would decrease it. However, the plot reveals risky location has a mostly neutral impact on lapse probability, with most places clustered around zero. The absence of a clear trend suggests the relationship between risky location and lapse risk likely varies across participants. Moreover, the self-reported risk level of a location is subjective, that is, what is high risk to some might be low to others. Now that we have a sense of how these features can predict lapse risk, let’s see how they vary across individuals and time.

Local Importance

  • Shapley Values

    • Negative values decrease lapse probability.
      • Can act as protective features


    • Positive values increase lapse risk probability.
      • Can act as risk features

Shapley values are an explanatory approach providing insight into the relative risk features for an individual. They allows us to determine which features on which days impact lapse risk for each participant. Negative values indicate a decreased risk of lapse, and can act as protective features. While positive values indicate an increased risk of lapse, and can act as risk features. Lets look at the most important features for a specific participant across the study.

Local Importance

In the beginning, this participant had little change in their risk features, with past use and future efficacy bringing down their probability of lapse. The other features are staying constant at zero, indicating little to no influence on lapse risk. However, around day 35, past use, the blue line, drives up their lapse probability considerably. This increase coincides with the slight increase in time spent at a risky location, as shown with the purple line. Risky location provides insight into why this individual may have lapsed, as they likely visited a bar and drank alcohol. Moreover, future efficacy, the yellow-green line, is shown closely lagging behind past use. This makes sense as a person who is currently using may feel less confident about future abstinence. Around day 50, the future risky situation feature decreases their lapse risk, acting as a protective feature at certain points. This participant likely reported a low likelihood of risky situations in the next week. Let’s now compare with another individual to highlight the need for personalized supports.

Local Importance

For this participant, past use acts as their most important protective feature across time, differing from the previous example. This is shown by the blue line bringing down their lapse risk, indicating someone who did not lapse on study. The green line increasing their lapse risk is location valence, or the emotions associated with a particular place. It’s likely that time spent at an unpleasant place increased this participant’s lapse risk. Around day 75, future efficacy jumped above location valence, indicating a higher likelihood of drinking, relative to previous self-reports. Their lack of confidence in future abstinence increases their probability of lapsing, surpassing location valence as the most important risk feature. There is a vast difference between this participant’s relative risk features and the previous. For this participant, past use acts as their most important protective feature, consistently lowering their risk of lapse. As opposed to the previous plot, where past use acted as the most important risk feature, increasing their risk of lapse. This highlights the need for personalized recommendations and supports as different risk features can impact lapse risk in different ways, at different times, for different people. The ability to use these features to deliver tailored interventions that support recovery is the first step towards clinical implementation.
